The Lost — Harrow — Appearance & Background
Fae Mien — Portrait

As seen by other changelings, in the Hedge, or when the Mask falls

Mortal Guise

A lean, weathered man who appears to be in his late forties. Dark, unkempt hair, slightly too long. Yellow eyes that catch light at the wrong angle. Teeth just sharp enough to notice if he smiles, which he rarely does. The kind of stillness that makes people uncomfortable without knowing why — not threatening exactly, just wrong in the way a room feels wrong before you identify what has been moved.

He dresses in layers: a heavy dark shirt, worn trousers, battered boots, and over everything a rough brown overcoat with the collar turned up regardless of weather. The coat is old. It has pockets he uses and pockets he does not open. He smells faintly of the Hedge — something green and cold and just past the edge of recognition.

Nobody looks at him twice in a pub. Nobody remembers him being there. That is entirely intentional.

Fae Mien

The human structure remains — brow, cheekbones, jaw — but the bloodhound bleeds through it. Skin deepened to red-brown, loose and heavily wrinkled, folding at the brow and around the mouth into something between grief and age. Long floppy ears hang heavily below the jaw, flat and skin-heavy, unmistakably canine. The nose broadens and darkens, nostrils wide and restless, reading the air constantly. Deep jowls. The eyes drop at the outer corners — full amber, no white visible, heavy-lidded and sorrowful, tracking everything.

Not a monster. Something patient and ancient wearing a man's face imperfectly. Built entirely for finding things — and never forgetting what it finds.

I. Mortal Life

The man who would become Harrow was a homicide detective in the city's Major Crimes unit. His name is gone now, burned out of him somewhere in Arcadia, but the work remains etched into his bones. He was good. Not brilliant, not the kind of detective who made leaps of intuition from nothing. He was the other kind: the grinder. The one who knocked on every door, re-read every statement, walked the crime scene until his shoes wore through. He didn't solve cases through genius. He solved them because he didn't stop.

That persistence was his undoing.

A young man was found stabbed to death in an alley behind a nightclub. The evidence pointed to a drifter named Marcus Boyle: eyewitness testimony, a partial fingerprint, a history of violence. The detective built the case methodically. He believed in it. Boyle went to prison for life.

Three years later, another body. Same MO. Same alley. Then another. The detective pulled the old files and started to see the cracks. The eyewitness who'd been nudged. The fingerprint that could have matched half a dozen people. The tunnel vision of a man who needed the case closed. Marcus Boyle died in prison fourteen months before the detective found the proof that would have exonerated him. Shanked in the yard over a pack of cigarettes. The detective kept the newspaper clipping in his wallet. A two-paragraph obituary for a man nobody mourned.

II. The Crossing

The detective was working the alley. The same alley, the one behind the nightclub where the first body had been found. He went back there most nights by then, half-drunk and fully obsessed, walking the scene like a man saying a rosary. As if the concrete would finally confess what he'd missed.

That night, the alley went on too long. He noticed. Of course he noticed. He was a detective, even a ruined one. The dumpsters repeated. The fire escapes multiplied. The light from the street behind him stretched thin and then vanished, and the air changed, and there was a door at the end of the alley that had never been there before. Old wood. Iron hinges. And behind it, the smell of something vast and authoritative. Like a courtroom. Like incense. Like the weight of a verdict about to fall.

He should have turned around. Any sane man would have. But the detective hadn't been sane for a long time, and the door didn't smell like answers. It smelled like relief. It smelled like a world where guilt wasn't a fog you choked on. Where it had a scent, clear and sharp and unmistakable, and you could follow it, and when you found the source you could bare your teeth and tear it out. No ambiguity. No second-guessing. No lying awake wondering if you'd sent the wrong man to die. Just the trail, and the hunt, and the certainty at the end.

He didn't think about it. His hand was on the door. The door was open. He was already through. Not because he was dragged. Not because he was tricked. Because he was a man drowning in his own guilt, and the door offered him the one thing no amount of whiskey ever could: the freedom of being a creature that doesn't doubt. A dog doesn't wonder if it's wrong. A dog just follows the scent.

The Magistrate of the Scarlet Assizes was waiting on the other side.

III. The Durance

What came next, Harrow doesn't remember clearly. No Beast does.

There are fragments. Vivid, disconnected, soaked in sensation, the way an animal remembers. The cold of stone floors under his hands and knees. The overwhelming reek of the court: old wood, dried blood, something sweet and rotting underneath like fruit left too long in the sun. The sound of chains. Always chains. The taste of copper on his tongue and the way his gums ached when he caught a scent.

He was the Magistrate's hound. The Nose of the Court. He doesn't remember it as a role he understood. He remembers it as being. The way a dog doesn't know it's a dog. It simply is what it is. The accused were brought before the court and he would breathe in and the world would become scent. Guilt smelled sour and metallic, like old blood and vinegar. Lies prickled in his gums. Fear was sharp and wet. Innocence had no smell at all, which is how he knew it was rare.

He would smell. He would bay. He would bare his teeth and snarl at the guilty. Sometimes he would bite, lunging at the condemned, tearing at them while the Magistrate's voice boomed overhead, handing down sentences that the hound didn't understand but could feel in the way the air changed. The scream of something being unmade.

He doesn't remember the punishments as horror. That's what haunts him now. At the time, they were just what happened after the hunt.

Years. Decades. The detective's mind sank beneath the animal's. His body changed: face loosening, teeth sharpening, ears lengthening. He forgot language. He forgot his name. He forgot Marcus Boyle. He forgot what it meant to be wrong, because a hound is never wrong. A hound just smells what it smells.

Almost. Somewhere deep in the animal fog, in a place the Magistrate's power couldn't quite reach, something stayed. Not a thought. Not a memory. Just a feeling — a dull, persistent ache, like a wound that wouldn't close. The faintest scent of newsprint and guilt that belonged to no one in the court. His own.

IV. The Escape

It happened during a routine session of the Assizes. A small trembling creature was dragged before the Magistrate — some wretched hobgoblin accused of stealing fruit from the Keeper's orchards. The hound was brought forward. The Magistrate's voice boomed, as it always did.

The hound breathed in.

The hobgoblin smelled like dirt and fear and hunger. It did not smell like guilt.

Something shifted. The hound's nostrils flared. Its head turned, not toward the accused, but toward the bench. Toward the Magistrate. And for the first time, through the animal fog and the years of obedience and the layers of ancient power, the hound caught a scent it had never noticed before. Faint. Impossibly faint. Buried under authority and incense and the overwhelming reek of the court itself.

The Magistrate smelled like guilt.

The hound didn't reason through it. Didn't think "the system is corrupt." The hound simply smelled what it smelled, and what it smelled was wrong, and the wrongness triggered something older and deeper than the Magistrate's conditioning. The same stubborn, grinding persistence that had once made a mediocre detective walk a crime scene until his shoes wore through.

The hound raised its head. Looked at the Magistrate. And barked. Not a word. Not "Guilty." A bark. Raw, animal, furious. The sound of a hound turning on its master. Then, in the same breath, before the sound had finished echoing off the dark wood walls, it lunged.

The bite was brief. One savage contact — teeth finding something beneath the robes, something that was not quite flesh but yielded like it — before the Magistrate's power crashed down like a hammer. The robes rippled. The red curtains shuddered. The entire court recoiled from the thing it had built, the instrument of judgment that had just turned itself on the judge.

The bark had said guilty. The bite was the sentence.

It lasted maybe three seconds. Then the hound bolted — smashing through the court doors, through corridors that tried to close around it, through the Hedge that tore at its flesh and its memory in equal measure. Running on all fours, no thought, no plan, just the blind animal imperative to run, following the faintest scent of exhaust fumes and rain on concrete through the Thorns.

The man came back slowly. In pieces. Crawling out of the Hedge on a city sidewalk, shaking, bleeding, with a mouthful of teeth that were too sharp and a head full of fog just beginning to clear. His first coherent thought in what might have been decades was a name: Marcus Boyle.

V. The Return

Ten years had passed. Maybe more. Harrow's sense of time is still unreliable, and he doesn't have access to his own records to check.

Nobody noticed he was gone. That's what the fetch is for.

The thing that wore his face kept working. It stayed at Major Crimes, kept the desk, kept the badge. It got sober — AA meetings, one day at a time. It closed cases. Not the case, not the real one, but enough others to maintain a respectable record. It made amends with his ex-wife. It did all the things the real man was too broken to do. Eventually it retired with a modest pension, a clean record, and the quiet respect of colleagues who remembered a decent detective who'd pulled himself together in the end.

It lives in the suburbs now. Community garden. Morning walks. The peaceful ending the real man never earned, lived out by something made of sticks and glamour and borrowed memories.

Harrow caught its scent once, early on. The wrongness of it — sticks and glamour and borrowed memories reeking of Faerie. He watched it from across the street for maybe thirty seconds. Then turned and walked away. The fetch can keep the name he can't remember. It can keep the house, the pension, the quiet suburban life. None of it matters. That man is dead, and Harrow isn't him.

The world moved on in other ways too. The detective had never been good with computers. Now the notebooks are gone and the databases are everywhere. People carry phones that are also cameras, maps, filing cabinets, and tracking devices. CCTV footage is digital. Case files live on servers he can't access and wouldn't understand if he could. The whole infrastructure of modern investigation — the part that isn't shoe leather and instinct — has moved into a world that might as well be Arcadia for how alien it feels.

He compensates. He works contacts — criminals, street people, other changelings — to get information a modern detective would pull from a computer in thirty seconds. He uses his nose where others would use cameras. He talks to stray dogs instead of checking social media. It works, mostly. But it means he's always one step behind in the mortal world, even as he's three steps ahead in the world of scent and fear and instinct.

VI. The Present

Harrow found the Autumn Court within weeks of his return. The Ashen Court suits him. They understand fear, they value knowledge, and they don't ask too many questions about what you did before you came back. He's made himself useful as an investigator and tracker, sniffing out problems for the local freehold. The work keeps him sharp. The work keeps him from thinking too hard about the fetch.

But the real work — the work that burns in his chest like a coal that won't die — is the case. The original case. The murder that Marcus Boyle went to prison for. The killer is still out there. Ten years older, ten years colder, ten years further from justice.

Harrow has something now that the burnt-out detective never had: he can smell guilt. He can talk to the stray dogs that roam the alleys where the bodies were found. He can sense what people fear, and fear is the thread that connects every killer to every crime. He can follow trails that no mortal investigator could even perceive.

He is going to find the real killer. He is going to look them in the eye. And then he is going to do what the Magistrate taught him to do.

Pronounce sentence.

Mental Attributes

Intelligence •• (Below Average)

He was never the brilliant detective. He was the grinder — the one who knocked on every door. Even before Arcadia, sustained analytical reasoning wasn't his strength. The Durance finished what nature started. Years as an animal with no language, no abstraction, no capacity for structured thought burned out the neural pathways that academic intelligence runs on. He cannot research. He cannot build logical chains from documents and data. He cannot hold a complex argument in his head long enough to dismantle it. Put him in front of a spreadsheet and the old animal fog creeps in at the edges of his vision.

The Beast curse compounds this. No re-rolls on Intelligence pools. The hound's brain hits a wall every time something requires the kind of deliberate, trained cognition that years as an animal simply erased.

Wits •••• (Exceptional)

This is the attribute that survived. This is what the Magistrate cultivated, sharpened, and refined across decades of service: the hound's ability to read what is happening right now, in this room, with this person, in this moment. Nothing escapes him. The wrong detail registers before he can name it. The lie catches in someone's face before they've finished telling it. The shift in a room's atmosphere — the slight change in breathing, the micro-tension in a jaw, the smell of fear beginning — lands on him like a physical sensation.

He thinks the way a predator thinks. Fast, present, reactive. He lives entirely in the present tense. He doesn't plan. He notices.

Resolve •• (Average)

He can hold focus on a task. He won't crumble easily. But he isn't immovable, and the persistence that defined him as a detective — the grinding, relentless quality that walked crime scenes until his shoes wore through — was never about willpower. It was about guilt. About a wound that wouldn't close. He kept working not because he was strong but because stopping meant sitting alone with what he'd done to Marcus Boyle.

That guilt still drives him. It isn't strength. It's weight. And weight, applied long enough in one direction, moves things.

Physical Attributes

Strength •• (Average)

The Magistrate's hound was built for finding, not fighting. Lean, long-limbed, capable of sustained effort over distance rather than explosive force. Harrow can handle himself — twenty years of police work and however many years in Arcadia left him comfortable with violence — but he doesn't win fights through power. He wins them by being three steps ahead before the first punch is thrown, or by not being there at all.

Dexterity •• (Average)

No particular grace. No quickness beyond the ordinary. The hound's body was built for endurance and scent-work, not speed or precision. He moves like a man who is always conserving something, always keeping something in reserve. He doesn't rush. Rushing is for creatures that don't know where their quarry is going. He knows. He can afford to be unhurried.

Stamina •• (Average)

He is lean and weathered and has slept rough in the Hedge for longer than he cares to calculate. He doesn't get sick often. He doesn't complain about cold or discomfort. He can walk for a very long time on very little. But he is not robust. He can absorb punishment up to a point, and past that point he folds like any other man. The body is a tool. He maintains it minimally and uses it practically.

Social Attributes

Presence • (Poor)

He makes no impression walking into a room. He is the man nobody notices, the one nobody remembers being there. This is not an accident. The Magistrate needed a hound that could move through crowds unseen, that wouldn't announce itself before the quarry could be cornered. The absence of presence is a cultivated quality.

He can reach for it when he needs to — the Beast's fae nature allows him to spend Glamour and feel something animal and magnetic move through him. But it isn't his. It feels borrowed. He uses it sparingly and it always costs him something he'd rather keep.

Manipulation ••• (Good)

This is where his social power actually lives. He doesn't charm people. He moves them without them noticing they've been moved. He asks the question that sounds like small talk and isn't. He creates the conditions in which people say what they weren't going to say, do what they weren't going to do, and believe it was their own idea.

Twenty years of interrogation rooms. However many years reading the Magistrate's accused. He knows how people work — what they're afraid of, what they want to believe about themselves, where the pressure points are. He applies force precisely and quietly, the way a surgeon uses a scalpel rather than a saw.

Composure ••• (Good)

He presents a still surface. His face doesn't give things away. Under pressure he holds — not because he feels nothing but because he learned, somewhere in Arcadia, that showing reaction is showing weakness, and weakness in the Magistrate's court was an invitation. He is very hard to read in return. High Composure means the tools other people use to read him — Empathy, Subterfuge — roll against his stillness and often slide off.

The stillness is real. The calm underneath it is not always.

Investigation •••• (Crime Scenes)

This is the one thing that survived completely intact. Twenty years of walking crime scenes, re-reading statements, knocking on doors that didn't want to open. The detective work is in his bones in a way that Arcadia couldn't reach, perhaps because it was never purely cognitive — it was always instinctive, physical, sensory. He reads a scene the way the hound reads a scent trail. The specialty in Crime Scenes means the bonus applies specifically to the grammar of violence: the language of blood patterns, forced entry, staged evidence, the small lies a scene tells when something has been moved.

On a live scene, rolling Wits + Investigation, he fields eight dice. Almost nothing in the mortal world is better equipped for that specific task.

Empathy ••• (Detecting Lies)

The other half of the investigative toolkit. He doesn't need someone to finish lying before he catches it — he catches it forming. A change in breathing rhythm. The fraction of a second too long before the answer. The way someone's eyes don't move the way they should when they're telling the truth. This is Wits-driven — reactive, present, instinctive — and pairs with the specialty to give him a specific edge in the exact context where he uses it most: the face-to-face, the interrogation that looks like a conversation, the quiet drink at a pub that is actually an interview.

Neither Investigation nor Empathy alone is as powerful as both together. One reads the scene. The other reads the person. Between them, very little stays hidden for long.

Intimidation ••

Not the loud kind. Not the kind that raises its voice or slams hands on tables. The kind that sits very quietly across from someone and says almost nothing, and somehow the silence is more frightening than anything words could carry. Two decades of interrogation rooms and however many years as the Magistrate's instrument of judgment. He knows how fear works from both ends — as a tool and as a weapon — and he uses it with the same precision he applies to everything else. Works best through Manipulation: targeted, specific, applied to the exact pressure point that will make this particular person fold.

Streetwise •• (Criminal Networks)

Twenty years working Major Crimes means he knows how criminal spaces operate — the hierarchies, the currencies of information, the unwritten rules about what you ask and how you ask it and who you never ask at all. He can move through those spaces without immediately marking himself as someone who doesn't belong. The specialty in Criminal Networks is the specific knowledge of who connects to whom, which is useful when modern databases are closed to him and the information he needs lives in people rather than files.

Animal Ken • (Canines)

He understands dogs. Not sentimentally — he was one, or close enough that the difference stopped mattering somewhere in the Magistrate's court. He knows how they think, what they smell, what they're trying to communicate when they communicate. The Beast blessing gives him 8-again on Animal Ken rolls. One dot means the formal knowledge is thin — he doesn't command them with authority — but the understanding runs deeper than the number suggests. He talks to the stray dogs in the alleys where the bodies were found. They tell him things CCTV cameras can't.

Stealth ••

The Magistrate's hound needed to move unseen until it was time not to be. He can go unnoticed when he chooses — not through any supernatural trick, just through the practised art of being unremarkable. He picks the right corner to stand in. He knows when to be still. He has learned that people see what they expect to see, and a weathered man in a brown coat in a pub corner is exactly what they expect, which means they don't see him at all.

Brawl •• (Bite)

He knows how to hurt people. Two decades of police work and the Durance between them left him comfortable with violence in a way that has nothing to do with enjoyment. The specialty in Bite is the Hunterheart's kith power made explicit — the unarmed attack that deals lethal rather than bashing damage. It's not his first resort. It's closer to his last statement. The moment before the bite in the Magistrate's court wasn't rage. It was verdict. He carries that quality into every physical confrontation.

What He Cannot Do

No Academics. No Computer. No Science worth mentioning. The Beast's −4 untrained penalty on Mental skills means these aren't just gaps — they're walls. Digital evidence, forensic reports, databases, research: the entire infrastructure of modern investigation that isn't shoe leather and instinct is effectively closed to him. He compensates through contacts and canines and the supernatural toolkit the Durance gave him. It works. But it means he is always one step behind in the world that lives on screens, even as he is three steps ahead in the world that lives in rooms and alleys and the spaces between what people say and what they mean.

He has no Firearms. No Drive beyond the ordinary. No Persuasion in the formal sense — he doesn't make cases, he creates conditions. These are not things he lost. They are things that were never part of what the Magistrate needed.
